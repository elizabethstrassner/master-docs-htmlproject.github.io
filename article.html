A Run for Their Money

Illinois Candidates for Governor Spend $12 Million in Q2, Pritzker Alone $9 Mill

By Elizabeth Strassner

The path to the Illinois Executive Mansion, where the governor lives. Credit: Katherine Johnson, Flickr.

The path to the Illinois Executive Mansion, where the governor lives. Credit: Katherine Johnson, Flickr.

Running for governor isn’t cheap.

Just ask the many candidates competing to unseat Illinois Gov. Bruce Rauner, who is running for reelection next year. According to the most recent quarterly expenditure reports filed with the Illinois State Board of Elections, candidates for governor, including Rauner himself, spent over $12 million in the second quarter of 2017 alone — and the general election isn’t even until November 2018.

While races against incumbent candidates are traditionally considered harder to win, many Democrats see a unique opportunity with Rauner, a Republican whose approval ratings are among the lowest of any sitting governor in the United States.

Despite his unpopularity, Rauner has a substantial amount of campaign cash: he donated $50 million of his own money to his re-election fund last December and received a $20 million donation from billionaire Ken Griffin in May. The Chicago Tribune expects the 2018 gubernatorial race to be “the most expensive contest in Illinois history.”

With more than a year to go until the general election, candidates on both sides have already begun to spend big.

So far, 11 candidates have filed disclosures with the Illinois State Board of Elections, according to its website; of these, seven submitted itemized expenditure reports for the second quarter (Q2), which refers to the time period between April 1 and June 30 of this year.

The filings help clarify which candidates have the resources to campaign, which is particularly useful given the crowded nature of the Democratic primary field. Of the seven candidates with available expenditure reports, five — Daniel Biss, Chris Kennedy, Ameya Pawar, J.B. Pritzker, and Gov. Bruce Rauner — spent more than $100,000 during the last quarter.

Of the seven candidates who filed itemized expenditure reports with the Illinois Board of Elections, Pritzker spent the most by far.

Rauner was the second-largest spender. According to election board data, he spent around $1.9 million last quarter, roughly 57 percent of which went to advertising and media spending.

The biggest spender, by an enormous margin, was Democratic candidate J.B. Pritzker, heir to the Hyatt hotel fortune, who has self-funded a large portion of his campaign. In Q2 alone, Pritzker spent over $9.26 million — about three times more than all other candidates combined.

Where is all that money going? $6.32 million of Pritzker’s spending went to a single recipient: the Philadelphia-based strategic media firm Shorr, Johnson, Magnus (SJM).

Pritzker's campaign spent the vast majority of its Q2 expenditures on payments to Shorr, Johnson, Magnus, a strategic media firm.

SJM has a reputation amongst left-leaning political candidates for creating powerhouse advertisements. A 2012 spot created by the firm, “Stage,” aimed to paint Republican presidential candidate Mitt Romney as a soulless businessman and was later ranked the “most effective” ad of that year by Ace Metrix.

The Pritzker campaign’s 17 Q2 payments to SJM were coded as “Media Consulting,” “Media Production” and “Media Buy.” The firm’s work with Pritzker may have included the eleven different YouTube spots produced by the campaign over the last quarter, as well as a television advertising blitz.

Pritzker makes his pitch to the people of Illinois with a high-production-value ad.

Attempts to improve Pritzker’s name recognition may not be necessary, however: his surname appears on some of Illinois’ most prized institutions, including the University of Chicago medical school, a park in downtown Chicago, and Northwestern University’s top-ranked law school.

But Pritzker isn’t the only candidate in the race with a famous, wealthy family — though his competitor has been much shier about spending money.

Pritzker's campaign expenditures significantly outpaced Kennedy's in Q2.

Chris Kennedy, the son of late Sen. Robert Kennedy and nephew of former President John F. Kennedy, announced his bid for governor in February. But Kennedy’s second-quarter spending, around $651,000 total, pales in comparison with Pritzker’s — perhaps because he assumes most Illinoisans already know his name.

“[Pritzker’s campaign is] running a little bit of a shock-and-awe campaign, and trying to push me out in the summer so that he doesn’t have to go through the vetting process,” Kennedy told Chicago Tribune reporter and podcaster John Kass in June. “And there’s no way that’s going to happen…I’ll have enough money to compete.”

If he plans to win the nomination, Kennedy has some ground to make up: Pritzker recently won endorsements from the AFL-CIO and Cook County Democrats, leading both the Chicago Tribune and the Chicago Sun-Times to refer to Pritzker as the “front-runner.”

There’s a lot of time between now and the March 20 primary, however, and Kennedy and the rest of the crowded Democratic field will likely seek to make the most of that time.

Daniel Biss, for example, intends to give Pritzker and Kennedy a run for their money. Biss, a state senator, raised over $1 million in Q2; on July 10, the campaign announced that it has over $2.3 million on hand.

“Some may be surprised that we’re competitive with established, brand-name candidates, but our fundraising totals confirm what we already know: people looking for an alternative to J.B. Pritzker…are looking to Daniel Biss,” said Biss for Illinois campaign manager Abby Witt in a statement issued by the campaign. According to state election board data, Biss spent around $264,000 in the second quarter, including roughly $85,000 on consulting, $40,000 on digital advertising, and $68,000 on payroll and payroll taxes.

The final candidate to spend significantly in Q2, Ameya Pawar, spent only $152,000 — roughly what Pritzker’s campaign spent on travel alone. But Pawar, who famously won a tight 2011 race for the alderman seat of Chicago’s 47th ward, seems unconcerned with his comparative lack of funds. “On my end, having $50,000 in the bank is nothing to be ashamed of. It doesn't mean I'm not supported in other ways,” he told DNAInfo in January.

Pawar’s lean Q2 budget went primarily to staff salaries, polling, and advertising, according to the campaign’s expenditure filing.

Candidates' expenditures, with Rauner and Pritzker removed.

One silver lining for Biss and Pawar: any Democrat capable of winning the party’s nomination may stand a decent chance of success in the general election. According to a poll conducted on behalf of the Democratic Governors Association on July 26, Rauner currently trails even a generic Democrat, 37 percent to 49 percent.

As the primary approaches, all candidates will be likely to increase ad spending, even as Pritzker’s personal wealth dwarfs that of the rest of the field. That increased name recognition — particularly for Biss and Pawar — could be critical for success.

The other candidates’ comparatively minor spending paints a rosy picture for J.B. Pritzker, but with six months until the primary, it is still anyone’s race.
